    Allied Losses

    Harold Kirkham was a Private in the Defence Platoon of the 4th Parachute Brigade (Service Number 3857484). He was born on 24 April 1914, son of Alfred and Elizabeth Ann Kirkham of Bolton, UK. His brother, Sidney was a Sick Berth Attendant with HMS Aggressive, a Royal Navy shore establishment. Sidney wrote to his brother on 24 September 1944, but the letter was returned on 18 October 1944, stamped in the field by what little remained of 4th Brigade HQ, It indicated that Harold Kirkham was reported missing. It is believed that this is the only existing item to have been stamped by the 4th Brigade office in the field following the Battle of Arnhem.

    Kirkham served with the 4th Brigade Defence Platoon, whose task was to secure the drop zone rendezvous point for the vast majority of 4th Parachute Brigade who were scheduled to arrive the following day.

    Franked with 2d plus 1/2d adhesive stamps cancelled by red machine cancel POST OFFICE / MARITIME MAIL.     The Defence Platoon was involved in heavy fighting during their advance from the DZ in the direction of Arnhem. Just before they could push on to the center of Oosterbeek, they got into a fierce fight in the woods along the Valkenburglaan. This place would later be known as 'Hacket’s Hollow'. Several members of the platoon were killed or wounded here.

    L/Cpl. H. Boorman wrote in a short account after the war “.…the first I saw of them [Dolaghan and Kirkham] was on the 17th Sept, Sunday afternoon, as they were leaving the D.Z. near Oosterbeek. I was a D.Z. casualty and was taken straight to a R.A.P…. later I heard from Pte. R. Burns that he was with Pte. Kirkham, when he got wounded in the legs and he was left in a wood near Oosterbeek….”

    In a letter dated 2 July 1945, Private Thomas Dolaghan described the fate of his friend Harold Kirkham: "…. I last saw Pte Kirkham about 10 minutes before I was taken prisoner. At that time we were withdrawing through some woods to a new position. He was fit and unwounded. I have since heard from Pte G. Bamborough that Kirkham was very badly wounded later on .... Kirkham asked Atkinson to leave him down as he thought he would not last much longer…"

    Harald Kirkham is buried in the Oosterbeek Airborne Cemetery in grave 28.B.I.

    Most likely the body of Harold was left behind where he died. After the war the process of locating field graves, the exhumation of the remains and reburial at the cemetery in Oosterbeek started.

    10. SS Panzer Division 'Frundsberg'

    SS-Sturmmann Herbert von Seelen had served with the SS missions in Serbia and the Soviet Union when he was promoted to SS-Unterscharführer (Deputy Squad Leader) on 19 April 1943, having completed an Artillery training course. From January to May 1943, he was serving in an Artillery unit in Germany after which he was transferred to the newly formed 10.SS-Panzer Division 'Frundsberg' based in Marseilles and North France. From April to June 1944 this division operated in Tarnopol (Ternopol) and Lemberg (Lviv) in Ukraine. The 'Frundsberg' Division then returned to France and fought during the Allied invasion of Normandy where it was decimated.

    The remnant of the Division was then transferred to Arnhem for Rest and Recreation, but instead they quickly became involved in defending against Operation Market Garden. The Division fought against British airborne troops throughout the Arnhem area. Herbert von Seelen was transferred to a Praguebased replacement unit on 24 September 1944, most likely due to an injury that he received during the Battle of Arnhem.

    Card received two primitive hand stamps: TERUG / AFZENDER (return / sender) on the front and the mutilated Geen Post / verbinding (no mail / connection) on the back.

    While Arnhem was the focal point of the battle, Maastricht had been liberated on 14 September 1944. From the content: “…the first guests are gone… the wildest rumors are making the rounds.… yesterday we were in the cellar 4 times… was in Eindhoven yesterday morning at 10am and by 7:30pm in Maassluis… had to flee from the train 9 times…”

    Excerpt from the card: “I received your note, it took a week. How is Cathy doing, did she stay there? I don’t think mail is still coming through… We no longer see any family because one can no longer travel. We have gas for a few hours but coal is reduced this week…”
