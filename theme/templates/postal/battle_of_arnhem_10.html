    Role of the Red Cross

    After Operation Market Garden Dutch railway personnel remained on strike for the remainder of the war. As a result, postal service was severely restricted because trains and postal vans were no longer available. The Red Cross was initially able to transport mail. Rheden (10 miles east of Arnhem) was situated in the German defense zone and the population had not been evacuated. Its Red Cross branch performed a crucial role carrying mail to those places where it could still be delivered locally. The Rheden Red Cross used two different hand stamps.

    On 17 September 1944, 30,000 Dutch railway workers went on strike, stopping all train and bus traffic, while the national postal service, PTT, was seriously hampered. The Red Cross in Rheden (GL) immediately began to transport mail until it was banned by the Germans on 14 October 14. All Red Cross vans were confiscated on 17 November 1944.

    Red Cross assistance with mail delivery often occurred without any involvement from the Dutch postal service (PTT). At other times, the Red Cross only delivered an item to the addressee at the end stage.

    German Red Cross Tracing Service

    These declarations were given by German soldiers returning home after the war from POW camps or prisons. In them they provided information about soldiers in their former units who were still classed as missing in action. These accounts date from 1957 and 1958.

    Schaffroth's testimony was given in connection to Gefr. (PFC) Martin Kessler, who was still recorded as missing. He testifies that:

    “Martin was seen in Thomas bunker near Goch …. Moved from there to another bunker that was then assaulted by the British with flamethrowers…. Presume Martin was burnt alive as he was not seen afterwards.”

    Goch is a German town in the district of Kleve, south east of Nijmegen, close to the Dutch border.
