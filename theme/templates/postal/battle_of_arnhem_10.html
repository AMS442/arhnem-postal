    Prisoner of War Mail

    Oflag 79 was a German POW camp for Allied officers. Many British officers were held there after they had been taken prisoner at Arnhem during Operation Market Garden. The camp was located at Waggum near Braunschweig, also known by its English name, Brunswick. It was situated in a three-story brick building that had previously housed a German parachute regiment, near the Hermann Göring aircraft engine factory.

    Sent from the Postüberwachung (Mail monitoring) in Stalag IV, to the senior British Officer in Oflag 79. The letter was checked and passed by the camp censor #28 who placed the boxed purple handstamp.

    The 43rd (Wessex) Division, part of XXX Corps, played a major role in Operation Market Garden. Leading elements of the Division arrived in Driel on the evening of Friday, September 22. An unsuccessful attempt was made that night to put elements of the Polish brigade across the Nederrijn (Lower Rhine). British and Polish engineers on both sides of the Rhine had worked through the day to improvise a crossing using small boats linked by signals cable but the cable kept breaking, forcing the Polish troops to row across against the strong current. The attempt was made under enemy fire and only 52 soldiers of the 8th Polish Parachute Company had made the crossing before a halt was called at dawn.

    Two companies from the 4th Battalion, The Dorsetshire Regiment, 43rd Division were put across the river, but the location of the crossing point was ill-advised and the Dorsets landed among German positions. Fragmented and immediately pinned down, only 75 of the 315 men who crossed reached the Airborne perimeter in Oosterbeek, with the remainder being taken killed or captured. As a result of this failure, it was decided to withdraw the 1st Airborne Division from its bridgehead on the northern side of the Rhine.

    In the aftermath of Market Garden, 43rd Wessex Div was stationed on the “island” between the Rivers Waal and Nederrijn, Overnight on 22–23 October 1944, men of the 43rd Div, together with Britain's MI9 intelligence organization, and the Dutch Resistance evacuated 138 men, mostly soldiers trapped in German-occupied territory who had been in hiding since the Battle of Arnhem a month earlier.

    Clifford (Cliff) James Taylor, born in 1917, was promoted to Lance Corporal in the 9th Platoon of A Company in the South Staffordshire Regiment on 1 August 1944. Posted to 1st Air Landing Brigade, he served as a Bren gunner during the Battle of Arnhem. On Monday, 18 September, the 2nd day of the battle, Taylor landed by glider at Drop Zone S near Reijerskamp in Wolfheze. Originally assigned to guard Drop Zone X for parachute troops coming on a later lift, their orders were changed and they were directed to head for Arnhem and the bridge. His regiment came through Oosterbeek and joined soldiers from the 11th and 3rd Parachute Regiment that gradually worked their way along the river and finally, from “high up” in a street lined with large houses, they could see the bridge at Arnhem - Target Mike One.

    Heavy fighting ensued for the next several days. Late on Tuesday, 19 September, the Germans attacked the South Staffs position with combined tanks and infantry. Without sleep, with little remaining food and drink, and almost out of ammo, Cliff and surviving members of his unit were captured in the top floor of a home located on Utrechtsweg, opposite the Museum, known forever afterwards to the South Staffs as the “Monastery”.

    Cliff Taylor and his fellow POWs were taken in trucks to a large shed near the railway line and then were put into freight cars, having first been made to remove their boots to prevent escapes. After arriving at Stalag XII A, located near Limburg, Cliff was assigned POW nr. 89432.

    Mrs. Taylor wrote to her son on 14 March 1945. She wrote about day-to-day activities, gardening and how much she looked forward to him coming home.

    Stalag IV C was co-located with a plant that processed oil from coal. As part of the Allied bombing campaign to interrupt German oil production, it was bombed on multiple occasions between July 1944 and April 1945. In the second raid on 21 July 1944, six British POWs were killed and 21 were injured.

    In September 1994 Cliff wrote the following: "I shall never forget the friendship and kindness of the Dutch people. .... we turned Oosterbeek and Arnhem into a battlefield and we brought them death and suffering - yet, when we return now, we find only friendship."

    Stalag IV C was liberated by the Russian Army on 15 May 1945. Cliff went by train and then truck to Pilsen, before being flown by the Americans to Rheims. He was de-loused on arrival and then given an American uniform, Lance Corporal Clifford Taylor could finally send this telegram home. His war was over.

    The Battle of Arnhem - Dutch Resistance

    Oflag 79 was a German POW camp for Allied officers. Many British officers were held there after they had been taken prisoner at Arnhem during Operation Market Garden. The camp was located at Waggum near Braunschweig, also known by its English name, Brunswick. It was situated in a three-story brick building that had previously housed a German parachute regiment, near the Hermann Göring aircraft engine factory.

    Lombeers was a Dutch member of the Intelligence Department active in Gelderland. “Authorities are kindly requested to grant him all the help and to give him all necessary admissions for duty-purposes."

    By the middle of 1944, there were four major Resistance organizations in Holland. They did not coordinate their activities and for the most part, they did not answer to a central headquarters. Many members of the resistance did not learn the identity of their particular group until after the war.

    OSS/SOE Jedburgh teams deployed with each Allied airborne division during Operation Market-Garden. The Jedburgh teams worked closely with their respective division commanders, performing civil affairs and unconventional warfare missions. However, they were primarily concerned with obtaining tactical intelligence provided by Resistance members.

    During Market Garden, intelligence supplied by the various Resistance networks was passed through the Jedburgh teams to the various tactical commanders. The commanders received intelligence on the composition and disposition of German forces, as well as information on terrain and condition of the bridges. Once the paratroopers were on the ground, this information flow continued.

    Some Resistance members carried out independent actions during the operation. Others actively sought out airborne soldiers and attached themselves to any unit that would take them. In cases where their loyalties were suspect, Resistance members were vetted by the Jedburgh teams. Once this was done, they were farmed out to different units. However, the British plan for using the Resistance fell apart after Col. Barlow, the officer in charge of civil affairs and of working with the Resistance in the Arnhem area, was killed.

    After the Oosterbeek perimeter collapsed, the resistance helped some 250 airborne soldiers, who were in hiding, escape across the Rhine, including 138 men in one night (22/23 October 1944) during Operation Pegasus.
