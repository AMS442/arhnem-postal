    The Battle of Arnhem - Lasting Thanks

    Despite the 1st British Airborne's failure to hold the Arnhem bridge and the resulting devastation incurred by the local population, memorial events have been held in Arnhem and Oosterbeek on the anniversary of the battle since 1945. These include wreath laying ceremonies, parades, concerts, and other commemorative activities, honoring all those who fought and died in the battle.

    Every September a joint Anglo-Dutch Service of Remembrance is held at the Oosterbeek War Cemetery, where local school children each lay a flower on the grave of a British paratrooper.

    The Airborne Museum Hartenstein in Oosterbeek is dedicated to the battle and tells the story from the perspective of the British airborne forces. It attracts many visitors interested in this important WWII battle.

    Books, films and TV programs about Operation Market Garden and the Battle of Arnhem continue to be produced, educating new generations about this battle. The 1977 film A Bridge Too Far brought wider attention to the battle.

    Arnhem today embraces its connection to the battle as part of the city's heritage, despite the destruction that it wrought. Streets and bridges are named after elements of the battle, keeping the memory alive. While it was a significant defeat for the Allies late in WWII and dashed hopes of an early end to the war, the story of holding out against the odds still captures interest. The battle epitomizes the courage of British airborne soldiers who held on heroically against a much larger German force. The battle has intrigued military historians due to the many controversies and "what ifs" about the plans and tactics. The debates keep scholarly attention on Arnhem.

    After hostilities ceased, children living in the vicinity of the battle were each assigned a grave in the Airborne War Cemetery in Oosterbeek to maintain. It was in the summer of 1945 when several hundred veterans attended the first commemoration, which has been repeated ever since. Over a thousand young people have laid flowers on the graves of the fallen soldiers in the annual remembrance ceremony. The Commonwealth War Graves Commission runs the cemetery which holds the remains of more than 1764 WWII casualties of the battle.

    This unofficial medal was issued to commemorate the 50th anniversary of the Battle of Arnhem, presented by the Town Council of Oosterbeek, Netherlands, to those involved in Operation Market Garden.

    Theirs Is the Glory was a 1946 British war film about the British 1st Airborne Division's involvement in the Battle of Arnhem. It was the first film to be made about this battle, and the biggest grossing UK war film for nearly a decade. The later film A Bridge Too Far depicts Operation Market Garden as a whole and includes the British, Polish and American Airborne forces, while Theirs Is the Glory focuses solely on the British forces, and their fight at Oosterbeek and Arnhem. Using the original locations of the battle, the film featured veterans who were actual participants in the battle. Though no credits appear before or after the film, over 200 veterans of the battle appeared as actors. The film was directed by Brian Desmond Hurst, who was himself a veteran of the First World War.

    The 506th Regiment, US 101st Airborne Division, was tasked with securing bridges and sections of highway near the city of Eindhoven. Landing on Drop Zone C, near the town of Son, the 506th's first objective was to secure Son's bridges to Eindhoven in order to provide a route for the British Armored Guards to quickly reinforce the units dropped further north.

    This photograph shows men of the US 506th Parachute Regiment assembling at the Merks farm in Sonniuswijk. PFC Park H. Appler of Company G is walking towards the camera, carrying an M1903 Springfield rifle (unusual for paratroopers at this date) adapted for grenade launching. The girl on the left looking into the camera is Betsie Pijnenburg, and the tall man in the dark jacket is Jan Pijnenburg.

    In 1994 the Dutch postal service selected Jones' picture for a special 50th Anniversary stamp. This led to a somewhat painful argument when two veterans - former PFC Appler of the 506th and former Staff Sergeant Arthur H. Bittner of Company A of the 326th Engineers - both claimed to be the man in the picture. However, since it is certain that the picture was taken on the 17th, and since Company A of the 326th only arrived by glider on the 18th (the 3rd platoon had parachuted in on the 17th, but at Veghel), it can only be PFC Appler.

    The 1st (Polish) Independent Parachute Brigade was a parachute infantry brigade of the Polish Armed Forces under the command of Major General Stanisław Sosabowski. The Poles were initially landed by glider on 18 September, while the parachute section of the Brigade was held up due to bad weather over England, and only jumped on 21 September at Driel on the South bank of the Rhine. The Poles suffered significant casualties during the next few days of fighting.

    The epic war film A Bridge Too Far was released in 1977. Based on a non-fiction book of the same name by historian Cornelius Ryan, the film was directed by Richard Attenborough and featured an all-star cast featuring Dirk Bogarde, James Caan, Michael Caine, Sean Connery, Edward Fox, Elliott Gould, Gene Hackman, Anthony Hopkins, Hardy Krüger, Laurence Olivier, Ryan O'Neal, Robert Redford, Maximilian Schell and Liv Ullmann.

    Gen. Sir John Winthrop Hackett, GCB, CBE, DSO & Bar, MC, commanded 4th Parachute Brigade at Arnhem. He was severely wounded in the stomach but managed to escape with the help of the Dutch underground.

    Hen Idenburg was a member of the Dutch resistance, who assisted with Gen Hackett's escape. Another 39 Allied soldiers and airmen followed the same escape route and arrived home safely.

    Dr. Elsa Caspers was a member of the resistance, who initially helped Jewish children avoid deportation. On February 3, 1945 she accompanied Brigadier Hackett on his escape as they traveled more than 50 miles (80 km) by bicycle.

    Koos Meyer , a former barge captain and member of the Albrecht resistance group, developed the escape route through the Biesbosch, a region of waterways, creeks and marshes, that was taken by Brig. Hackett on the night of 4/5 February 1945. The Albrecht group made 374 clandestine crossings; Koos participated in 29 of them.

    Flight Lieutenant David Lord captained Dakota KG374 on 19 September 1944. This was his third mission over Arnhem. Once over Arnhem, the plane was immediately hit by flak and the starboard engine burst into flames. Lord, hearing that they were only 4 minutes out from the drop zone, decided to continue with the mission. The aircraft was again hit and the starboard wing caught fire. All but two panniers of ammunition were dropped from the blazing Dakota, so Lord flew around a second time to deliver the remainder. Flying Officer King was thrown clear of the plane and safely landed by parachute, but the remaining 3 crew members and 4 Army despatchers were killed as the aircraft crashed in flames. Fit Lt Lord was posthumously awarded the Victoria Cross for “supreme valor and self sacrifice”.
