    17-25 September 1944
    False Hope and Lasting Thanks

    On 6 June 1944, Operation Overlord saw Allied forces engage in the largest amphibious invasion in history. By June 30, over 850,000 men had landed on the Normandy shores and the Allies had begun to steadily move northwards. By the end of August, German forces had withdrawn across France and the Low Countries towards the German border.

    On 17 September, the First Allied Airborne Army and XXX Corps of the Second British Army launched Operation Market Garden, the largest airborne operation of the war. Its objective was the creation of a bridgehead, stretching from the Belgian border and over the Rhine River, a distance of 64 mi (103 Km), thereby providing a route into Northern Germany. U.S. and British airborne forces were to simultaneously capture and hold 9 bridges across 6 major waterways while XXX Corps would swiftly follow overland and secure the invasion route.

    The plan of action consisted of two operations:
    Market: Airborne forces of the First Allied Airborne Army to seize bridges and other terrain, under tactical command of Lieutenant General Frederick “Boy” Browning, and
    Garden: Ground forces of the Second Army to move north spearheaded by XXX Corps under Lieutenant-General Brian Horrocks. The first elements of the British 1st Airborne Division landed at midday on 17 September 1944, some 8 miles (13 km) from its objectives. At first the troops made excellent progress, being welcomed as liberators by the jubilant Dutch civilians. However, its advance towards the road and railway bridges was soon hampered by unexpected resistance, especially from elements of the 9th and 10th SS Panzer Divisions.

    The first elements of the British 1st Airborne Division landed at midday on 17 September 1944, some 8 miles (13 km) from its objectives. At first the troops made excellent progress, being welcomed as liberators by the jubilant Dutch civilians. However, its advance towards the road and railway bridges was soon hampered by unexpected resistance, especially from elements of the 9th and 10th SS Panzer Divisions.

    Only a small force of approximately 750 men was able to reach the Arnhem road bridge, while the Germans were able to blow up the railway bridge before it could be captured. The main body of the division was stopped on the outskirts of the town. XXX Corps was unable to advance north from Nijmegen as quickly as planned and after four days, the small British force at the bridge was overwhelmed and what was left of the division became trapped in a small pocket in the village of Oosterbeek, just north of the river. After nine days of fighting, the remnants of the division were withdrawn across the Rhine in Operation Berlin.

    The 1st Airborne Division lost nearly three quarters of its strength and did not see combat again. The Allies were unable to advance further with no secure bridges over the Rhine and the front line stabilized south of Arnhem, extending the war in the North of Holland for another 8 months. This split between north and south severed postal and other connections. Despite its defeat, the enduring battle of the British 1st Airborne Division at Arnhem has become the stuff of legends and the subject of numerous books, as well as the popular film “A Bridge Too Far.”

    Allied Forces

    Although most Allied commanders favored a broad front policy to continue the advance into Germany, Field Marshal Bernard Montgomery approached General Dwight “Ike” Eisenhower, supreme commander of Allied forces in western Europe, with an uncharacteristically bold plan to head north through Holland on a narrow front, bypassing the German Siegfried Line and opening a route into the Ruhr, the industrial heartland of Germany. If successful, the plan would open a “back door” to Germany and hopefully force an end to the war in Europe by year’s end.

    Initially proposed as a British and Polish operation codenamed Operation Comet, the plan soon expanded to include most of the First Allied Airborne Army with a simultaneous ground advance into the Netherlands by the British Second Army, led by XXX Corps under Lieutenant-General Brian Horrocks. The plan was codenamed Operation Market Garden. Lieutenant - General Frederick “Boy” Browning was given overall command of the airborne operation.

    Montgomery’s plan required the seizure of several bridges across the Maas, the Waal and the Nederrijn (Lower Rhine) rivers, as well as several smaller canals. The U.S. 101st Airborne, under Major General Maxwell Taylor, would drop just north of Eindhoven, capturing the bridges at Son and Veghel, while the 82nd Airborne, under Brigadier General James Gavin, would drop northeast of them to take the bridges at Grave and Nijmegen.

    The task of securing the road, rail and pontoon bridges over the Nederrijn at Arnhem fell to the 1st Airborne Division under the command of MajorGeneral Roy Urquhart, with the Polish 1st Independent Parachute Brigade attached, under Major General Stanislaw Sosabowski. The division was made up of three brigades of infantry (two parachute, one glider-borne), supported by Royal Engineer units, artillery of the 1st Airlanding Light Regiment, and elements of the Royal Army Service Corps and Royal Army Medical Corps. While most of the division had seen action in North Africa and Sicily, particularly the 1st Parachute Brigade and 1st Airlanding Brigade, this was the first time that the division had fought as a complete formation.  This force was also supported by some 1,200 men of the Glider Pilot Regiment, who would fly the glider-borne infantry and vehicles into Arnhem, providing the equivalent of two battalions of infantry. 

    From the beginning Urquhart was severely constrained in his planning for the operation. US IX Troop Carrier Command, under Major General Paul Williams, would not land all the airborne troops at one time, having decided that it would only be possible to fly one air lift per day. This meant that it would take three days to deliver the entire division. Few areas were suitable for glider landings and Gen Williams was reluctant to send his aircraft too close to Arnhem, concerned by anti-aircraft flak from nearby Deelen airfield. Urquhart was forced to pick drop zones (DZ) and landing zones (LZ) up to 8 mi (13 km) from Arnhem, on the north side of the river. With the need to secure the bridges, towns and drop zones for subsequent supply drops, the 1st Airborne would need to defend a perimeter 18 mi (29 km) long whilst waiting for XXX Corps. This was to prove a daunting task.

    German Forces

    Although most Allied commanders favored a broad front policy to continue the advance into Germany, Field Marshal Bernard Montgomery approached General Dwight “Ike” Eisenhower, supreme commander of Allied forces in western Europe, with an uncharacteristically bold plan to head north through Holland on a narrow front, bypassing the German Siegfried Line and opening a route into the Ruhr, the industrial heartland of Germany. If successful, the plan would open a “back door” to Germany and hopefully force an end to the war in Europe by year’s end.

    In early September, the II SS Panzer Corps, under the command of Obergruppenführer Wilhelm Bittrich had moved into the area north of Arnhem to refit and reorganize.  It comprised the remains of the 9th SS Panzer Division Hohenstaufen, under the command of Standartenführer Walter Harzer and the 10th SS Panzer Division Frundsberg under Brigadeführer Heinz Harmel. Although badly mauled in Normandy, the corps was made up of veterans and had significantly more forces available than the Allies had been led to expect. The divisions were also specially trained in antiairborne operations.  The exact number of men who were available after the withdrawal from Normandy is unclear, with estimates ranging from 6,000 to 10,000 men.

    The card was addressed to Konrad Bodzanowski, who, with his wife Helen, used to own a ladies apparel store in Berlin. They had a daughter, Hana, who donated the extensive family archive to the U.S. Holocaust Memorial Museum in Washington, DC after the war (Hana Wieder Collection 1926 –1956). In Hennie Birnbaum’s card to Konrad in Brussels she asks, “How are you two and your dear children doing?” suggesting that Konrad and Helena had at least two children. Further investigation has not shown what happened to the Bodzanowski family (but it is known that Konrad received a letter in Israel in 1956 advising him of the circumstances of his mother Regina’s death.)

    Civil Internment Camps

    The Indies Hostages

    After the Dutch surrender to the Germans in May 1940, the Dutch East Indies remained free and unoccupied. German citizens living in the colony were arrested and interned to the outrage of German authorities.

    In retaliation, 400 Dutchmen were arrested in Holland. They became known as the Indische Gijzelaars, the Indies Hostages. The Germans hoped that by arresting people who were on leave from the colony or had close ties with it, they would ensure proper treatment of the German internees. For this reason, the Indies Hostages were relatively well treated. Unlike the majority of civilian prisoners, they were allowed to receive Red Cross parcels, clothes and blankets. The Dutch hostages were initially taken to Zivilkonzentrationslager Buchenwald, but in November 1941, they were moved to Haarendael Major Seminary in the town of Haaren. They were then moved to Beekvliet Minor Seminary in St. Michielsgestel and then to a boarding school for boys called De Ruwenberg before finally being sent to Camp Vught. Camp Haaren became the generic name for the three Dutch locations where the hostages were held.

    In 1942, another group of hostages, civilian internees, were interned at Beekvliet. They were well-known scientists, politicians, government officials and artists. The Germans expected to use these additional hostages to deter the Dutch underground from acts of resistance. Thus, these internees were referred to as “anti-resistance hostages:” However, when the resistance nevertheless carried out an attack on a Nazi troop train outside Rotterdam, the Germans executed five of these hostages on August 15, 1942.

    Incoming and outgoing camp mail was free of postage but had to pass through the SD, the Security Police in nearby s’Hertogenbosch. Internees were allowed to send one letter per week.

    The Dutch Resistance

    Archival records show that Pieter Dirk Boyenk born in The Hague on April 26, 1918, was a resistance fighter. This undated letter was written to his mother, Maria Elisabeth Boyenk-Bos, born July 28, 1883, married to Giles Nicolaas Boyenk, born August 5, 1878. Pieter was arrested by the Germans and detained in Cell # 346 at the infamous Hotel Orange.

    Franciscus J. Brehm, b. December 23, 1918, was a resistance fighter arrested in Rotterdam and detained in the local prison on May 5, 1944. He was subsequently transferred to Camp Amersfoort, and it is known that Brehm was moved to Zwolle on September 26, 1944. The card was mailed in Borne, some 50 km southeast of Zwolle, on Wednesday, November 15, 1944, and because it is dated “Tuesday” on the card, he probably started writing it the day before. Brehm addressed the card to his family in Rotterdam.

    Well Alie, it is as I said. At the moment we are approaching the German border, so you know what is going on. Nasty isn’t it. But there is nothing that one can do about it. Let us hope for the best. I probably won’t be home now until after the war. If only we end up in one piece. Give my regards to everyone. Ignore the handwriting. We are writing in the freight train, and then we will throw it out.

    While in the Fair Building (Utrecht) I was asked about my profession I had. My answer was insufficient for my release… The German Major said: I have made a decidision and that is “labor”. We left at 11:30AM and arrived in the infamous Camp Amersfoort at 5:00AM. Although I know that you will try everything possible, I don’t think that you will get me released. I only hope that I survive... Say hi to the boys, and thank Mrs. Kloche for her kindness. I accidentally kept the keys to the safe in my pocket.

    It is clear that the sender, T. Kruijff, had this card with him in case he was arrested. It was thrown from the train en route from Camp Amersfoort and picked up and delivered after being found alongside the tracks. The stamp is not canceled, and it was delivered outside the postal system. He also added in pencil “ik ben opgepakt”, "I have been arrested." Then he added one more note at the bottom: a co-victim asked that his wife be notified and that all is fine so far.
