    The Battle of Arnhem and Its Impact on Civilian Life

    According to the Oosterbeek 1942 phone book, the widow J. Haartsen-Heeck lived at Utrechtseweg 54. Utrechtseweg 69 was listed as the house and shop of Mr. H.J. Eichner, a local bicycle and household goods store. It is not clear why the letter was addressed to nr. 69 instead of nr. 54. The house was built in 1910 and In 2020 the house was razed and the site was redeveloped.

    The writer, H.C. Maats, lived c/o Mrs. van Hengel, having been evacuated from Arnhem to Velp. From the content:

    "We were driven from our home on 24 September, not knowing if we shall ever return. When we were permitted to go return to Arnhem for 1 1/2 hours we saw how everything had already been turned upside down and how carpets, clothing, suitcases, appliances and the entire content of my linen cabinet had disappeared.... There was fighting in the center of town ... We had plans to go to Eerbeek, but got no further than Velp .... 30,000 evacuees arrived here but 20,000 had to move on because of the limited food supply.

    Severed Connection to Arnhem - Returned Mail

    The village of Bemmel (GL), located just 6 miles (10 km) north of Nijmegen saw heavy fighting. On 24 September, the British 69th Infantry Brigade was charged with liberating Bemmel along with two other villages in the area, One of its units, the 5th Battalion East Yorkshire Regiment, entered Bommel on 25 September but it would take until 28 September before the village was firmly in Allied hands.

    Although efforts to deliver mail continued despite the fighting, connections with smaller post offices often became impossible as they were either closed or unreachable. There are countless varieties in the hand stamps that were used to indicate the piece was to be returned to the sender.

    Both Lunteren and Ede are located in the province of Gelderland (GL), about 30 mils northwest of liberated Nijmegen. Having been so close to freedom, Lunteren and Ede would have to wait until April 1945 for liberation. Because they remained under German control the mail connection was restored:

    From the content:

    “I noticed that the connection was restored, so, when will we hear something from you? On my birthday we were waiting for you in vain… it is very scary here, the nonstop stream of evacuees, night and day, all their possessions on all kinds of vehicles, one has to weep, all this misery…”

    Although the volume of mail traffic remained fairly consistent during the German occupation (about 1 billion pieces per year), the speed with which the mail items were delivered decreased significantly in 1944. This was mainly due to the scarcity of fuel. As a result, vans were no longer motorized and mail was delivered by horse and wagon and postmen on foot.

    Renkum was ordered to evacuate by the Germans on September 23. The card was returned to Terwolde with the elusive hand stamp: GEEVACUEERD / ADRES ONBEKEND / TERUG (evacuated / address unknown / return).
