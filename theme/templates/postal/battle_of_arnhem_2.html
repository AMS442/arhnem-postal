    Mad Tuesday: 5 September 1944
    Dutch Collaborators Escape to Germany

    Dolle Dinsdag (Mad Tuesday) is the Dutch name given to Tuesday, 5 September 1944. On this day, rumors spread that liberation by Allied forces was at hand. On 4 September 1944, the Allies had finally liberated Antwerp and many Dutchmen prepared to receive the liberators. Unfortunately, the Allied advance came to a halt in southern Netherlands and the rest of Holland would not be liberated until May 1945.

    Allied Advance

    After Mad Tuesday, many Germans and Nazi sympathizers fled east to Germany believing a German defeat was near. On Wednesday, 6 September a train filled with women and children of NSB members headed towards Germany. Outside Amsterdam the train came under fire from Allied planes, resulting in 30 dead and many seriously wounded.

    To maintain contact with NSB members who had escaped to Germany, the Deutsch Niederlȁndische Gesellschaft Evakuiertenbetreuung created a service in Hengelo (OV) that operated until the end of the war. Mail was censored by the SS Zweigstelle NordWest.

    The Battle of Arnhem

    Sunday, 17 September saw 500 gliders and 1,500 aircraft with the British 1st Airborne Division taking the northern of two routes. The aircraft armada stretched 94 miles (150 km) in length and 3 miles (5 km) in width. Fighter escorts, consisting of 371 Spitfires, Tempests and Mosquitos, protected the fleet of aircraft.

    The 21st Independent Parachute Company jumped first at 12:40 to mark the landing zones for the main force of gliders and parachutists. Among the 180 men and 6 officers who dropped in with the 21st Independent Company, there were 21 Jewish refugees from Germany and Austria who had somehow managed to escape the Nazis and had joined the 1st Airborne Division. All but 5 traveled under assumed names so as to avoid execution as both a traitor and a Jew if captured. Four of them had already spent time in a concentration camp and most had not heard from family and friends for years. Five men from this group were killed during the Battle of Arnhem. The subsequent landings were largely unopposed and the battalions were formed up in good order and ready to carry out their tasks by 14:45.

    Lieutenant Peter Allenby jumped into Arnhem with the 1st Lift, on Sunday, 17 September 1944, with 117 men of the 16 Para Field Ambulance RAMC (3 additional 16 Para men came by glider). He wrote this letter to his wife from the C-47 Dakota aircraft en route.

    “Well, excuse what will inevitably be bad writing but this is it! We took off 20 minutes ago and I am sitting in the plane exactly opposite the door. Poor Charles sitting next to the door became rather quiet after a few minutes, then suddenly called for a “bag, grease proof paper, vomit, paratroops for the use of”. Six others have been sick already…. Darling, the sky seems to be full of planes …. I’ve never felt so calm & collected before a jump and this time we are going into action…. I must stop writing now and start to tie on my kit bag onto my leg…”

    The Battle of Arnhem

    The 1st Parachute Brigade, comprising the 1st, 2nd and 3rd Parachute Battalions and commanded by Brigadier G.W. Lathbury, landed with little fanfare. The three parachute battalions formed up and disappeared into the Dutch countryside, leaving members of the South Staffords, 1st Battalion Border Regiment and 7th Battalion Kings Own Scottish Borderers to secure the landing zones for the balance of the Division who were scheduled to arrive the next day. By 15:00 two platoons of the South Staffords, together with a section of Glider Pilots cleared the nearby village of Wolfheze.

    Private Robert Edwards (Service Nr. 5118991) served with 21 Platoon D Company, 2nd Battalion, South Staffordshire Regiment, 1st Airlanding Brigade. D Company, led by Major J.E. Phillip, arrived by glider on LZ S at approximately 13:30 on 17 September.

    By that evening troops of the 1st Parachute Brigade were locked in a desperate battle to break through German strongpoints ringing the western perimeter of Arnhem. Only the 2nd Battalion, under Lt. Co. John Frost, following the lower “Lion” route had reached the road bridge over the Rhine, while the 1st and 3rd Battalions, on the more northern “Leopard” and “Tiger” routes, were held up by the 16th SS Training Battalion under Sept Krafft. The following morning, Maj. Gen. Urquhart and Brig. Lathbury were cut off and surrounded in forward positions, and command of the division passed to Brig. Hicks. Seeing that the parachute brigade was bogged down as it attempted to reach the bridge, Hicks sent the South Staffords and the 11th Parachute Battalion into the city to support the attack.

    By 19:00 D Company of the South Staffs, the lead element, had taken up positions north of the Utrechtsweg, in and around houses west of the St. Elizabeths hospital. At 04:30 on the 19th September, the company took the lead as the 340 remaining members of the regiment advanced. The company reached the open space east of the hospital when suddenly, all hell broke loose. Private Edwards recalled “We must have looked like targets in a shooting gallery. All Jerry had to do was line up his guns on this one gap and fire…He couldn’t miss.” Despite the heavy fire, the South Staffs advanced about 300 yards before heavy losses forced them to ground. About 60 percent of the company had become casualties.

    Soon British positions around the museum were in danger of being overrun. Orders were given to evacuate. It was now every man for himself and the few remaining South Staffs fell back to the gardens and houses of Oosterbeek for the next 5 days. From 21-25 September most of the men had nothing to eat and little to drink. The divisional perimeter was under almost continuous fire from mortars and selfpropelled guns that gradually moved closer.

    The decision to evacuate the Oosterbeek perimeter was made on 24 September and “Operation Berlin” was put into action the next evening. The perimeter started thinning out after dark and the river crossings started at 22:00. The Glider Pilots acted as guides for the troops, who travelled down two routes towards the Rhine. All of the medical staff and chaplains remained behind to tend to the wounded.

    The South Staffs had gone into battle with 47 officers and 820 other ranks. Only 6 officers and 133 enlisted men returned. Private Edwards was one of the fortunate ones, returning across the river with Operation Berlin.
