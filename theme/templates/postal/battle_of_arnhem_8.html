    The Battle of Arnhem: Evacuees (continued)

    After it became clear that the Allies were failing to meet the objectives of Operation Market Garden, the Germans authorities issued orders to evacuate the entire town of Arnhem, starting with the area south of the bridge on 21 September and then the old town, north of the river on 23 September. Some families had already left when the fighting started but now almost 100,000 Arnhem citizens were forced to leave the town within two days. Meanwhile, the fighting would continue until the morning of 26 September.

    Even after the evacuation of Arnhem in September 1944, many houses still suffered from bombardments and artillery fire. The street in the address, Burgermeester Weertstraat, had many destroyed houses (below left) but Mr. Borgman’s residence at nr. 67 was completely restored after the war.

    After the liberation of Arnhem (codenamed Operation Anger) by Canadian forces on 14 April 1945, refugees were gradually allowed to return home, but the repatriation efforts would continue for months. Some residents only returned in September 1945. It would take years to rebuild the city.

    The Arnhem to which evacuees returned in 1945 was unrecognizable. The majority of residences were empty, having been plundered or destroyed.

    Excerpts from an undated circular issued by the Arnhem City government to former citizens returning home after the war:

    “The reconstruction of the town of Arnhem has now progressed to the point where you can return to your residence with your baggage….In order to expedite the return of the population of Arnhem as much as possible no repairs have been carried out in the home and it needs to be accepted in the current condition….

    In most cases it must be assumed that in the homes no bedlinens, stoves, and essential domestic goods are present. We point out, that before you can enter the town … all Arnhem evacuees have to pass through the medical control post set up in the Kleuterschool (Nursery school) ….. There an examination will be conducted as to infection with vermin, scurvy, etc. If there is a reason to assume this medical examination will prevent admission to the city, the evacuees are urged to first consider getting treated in the evacuation community and also to disinfect their goods…. No distribution articles can be obtained without proof of medical examination. Due to ongoing problems with the food supply, it is seriously recommended to bring food for a few days .…”

    Delayed Delivery - Überroller

    (Mail posted during one regime, then confiscated and processed by another)

    The letter originated in St. Antoniushove Hospital in Voorburg, where the writer and mother of the addressee was hospitalized. When it was found that her daughter's family had been evacuated from Arnhem, the letter was returned to the mother's home.

    Heijenoordsche Weg in Arnhem was located just north of the Arnhem-Utrecht railroad line. Many homes in the area were destroyed but contemporary photos shows that Nr. 6 was not damaged. It still exists today.

    Excerpts from the 5 page letter:
    Heard all the news about Arnhem … fortunately the V1 is finished, that was a horror every afternoon. Sunday they really hit it hard, the hospital was shaking as all the munitions exploded and the phosphor haze was scary… Just now the doctor stopped by and told me that the Arnhem and Nijmegen bridges are in Allied hands.”

    In the Arnhem 1942 address book, the resident of Thorbeckestraat 2 (right), is listed as the widow S.W.B Lely and the addressee of the card, Miss E.L. Frings, might have rented a room. The card was intercepted by the advancing Allies and held back in the Heerlen post office until February 1945. It then passed through the Eindhoven censorship office and after 25 May 1944 an attempt was made to deliver it to Arnhem. After the location was found to have been 'verbrand' (burned down), it went 'retour Heerlen', as indicated by the handwritten note.

    Heerlen was still under occupation and the card was confiscated by the advancing Allies and returned to the sender.

    The addressee's mother writes in part: "…Here everything is quiet and we just have to wait. Yesterday the noise was a constant, one knows very little but the rumors are plenty… such a comfort for me to know that you are not in A(rnhem) all by yourself…”

    The parents lived at Caumerdalschestraat 15, in Heerlen, a semi-detached house that still exists. The father was the principal of the 'Mijnschool' Mine School for coal mining engineers.

    Boxmeer is situated in the corridor between Nijmegen and Eindhoven and during Operation Market Garden those towns were liberated on 17 and 18 September 1944 respectively. With the simultaneous start of the national railway strike, transport of people as well as mail came to a temporary halt. This letter was enroute, before being intercepted by British troops and forwarded to the censor in England where it was opened, checked, resealed with the British label, and held for the time being.

    Boxmeer was liberated on 26 September and immediately declared to be a “front area”. This designation, which lasted for 5 months, restricted all traffic in and out of Boxmeer. It is not possible to know when the letter was finally delivered, even after March 1945.

    It appears the letter did not leave Rotterdam immediately after being posted and wound up in the middle of the hostilities. Boxmeer was liberated on September 26, 1944 by a small contingent of the Inns Court Regiment, nicknamed The Devil's Own. Led by Lt Angus Mitchell, eight men and three armored cars entered the village to find that the Germans had withdrawn. Lt Mitchell and his men moved through the town and went down to the River Maas, where they were fired upon from German forces on the other side. The men spent most of the winter in Boxmeer before crossing the Rhine the following spring.
