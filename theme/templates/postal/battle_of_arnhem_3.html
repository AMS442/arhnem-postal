    The Battle of Arnhem and Its Impact on Civilian Life

    The experiences of the Roza family in Oosterbeek during Operation Market Garden mirror those of others who were displaced by the fighting. They managed to escape the carnage and wound up as evacuees in Apeldoorn to the north. Huib Roza was located in the Arnhem 1942 address book, where he was described as a “deurw.” i.e., a “deurwaarder” or bailiff.

    Letter to Huib Roza, evacuated in Apeldoorn, from his brother who still lived in the village of Schaarsbergen with his family. By Wednesday, 20 September 1944 the German forces had begun to push at the eastern Oosterbeek perimeter defenses. A dozen men of 6 Platoon, B Company, KOSB were stationed at the corner of Cronjeweg and Van Dedemweg with a 6 pounder anti-tank gun of the 1st Airlanding Anti-Tank Battery. Their task was to prevent any infiltration from Stationsweg or the open parkland around Huize Dennenkamp opposite. Any infiltration would lead directly down to battalion HQ of the KOSB at the Dreijeroord Hotel (the White House), the stronghold of the KOSB.

    The Germans were shooting at the English, who were deployed in Huisze Dennenkamp, right through the Roza house. This map from 1936 shows the situation. The purple house is Huub Roza's residence, nr. 50, and adjacent to the right, in red, are numbers 48 and 46.The Roza's house went up in flames on Tuesday night, 19 September.

    Excerpts from the content: “Tuesday night at about 6pm our house was on fire after having been shot at. ....."D"' (Duitsers/Germans) were in de 'Spoorberg' (railway), while the 'E' (Engelsen/English) were in the Dennenkamp, and they were shooting at each other straight through our house. Furthermore the 'E' were re-supplied by plane. A container of gasoline landed on Z's roof and was shot through, causing a leak. The gasoline streamed over the burning roof so it burned like hell. The Germans were in our house with machine guns on the stairs to the cellar and the English were a few houses down and in the Annastrat. ..... all the houses in the neighborhood were heavily damaged. Every store in the area was converted into a machine gun nest, .... It was horrendous.

    6 Platoon advanced to the crossroad. It was not long before two German tanks/self-propelled guns moved south towards them from the Oosterbeek Hoog station direction, stopping some 40 yards short of the British position. The Germans then brought their tanks slowly down Stationsweg before turning west into Cronjeweg, continuing their advance. It is testament to the anti-tank gunners' camouflage that the German tanks had travelled more than 50 yards in plain sight of their gun and was now only a short distance from it but still had not seen it. The first tank was 'brewed up' with 3 rounds. The second tank could go nowhere - the road was blocked. The German infantry began to retreat under effective fire from 6 platoon and the anti-tank gunners.

    Excerpts from the content:

    “There was heavy shooting but miracle of miracles we arrived without a scratch in the cellar of W. van Zanten, wallpaper hanger, on Weverstraat. There we stayed from Tuesday evening to the next Monday night about 6 o'clock, so almost a week. We were there with 23 people …. We managed but it was very cramped.”

    “In one day we were taken out of the cellar 4 times by Germans who were looking for Englishmen. You could not stand it much longer. With the entire group we went via the Zweiersdal up on the Vredeberg… That is where we stood when the heavy bombardments took place.”

    “The village was one big mess, planes being shot at, in the Utrechtseweg not one tram tower was left standing. At last we arrived at the Apeldoornseweg….. The next morning Riek found space on a farmer's cart and she had hardly sat down before 2 planes shot at it. Freed was shot through his groin, Truus Köhler through her arm, others dead or wounded. Riek received a burn on her face but that has now healed. We continued. En route we were attacked 3 more times. We are now in the Elburgerweg 69, Apeldoorn and are doing fine. Riek is all set, a baby outfit has been assembled again”.

    “Rev. v. Ernst, v.d. Poel, Slettenaar, eldest daughter O. van Buren, Emme and Wife, the wife of the plumber Gaasbeek, Corrie Roessink (van Kaasom) are all dead.”

    Huib Roza used a German field post card to tell his family:

    “Oosterb Apeldoorn, 21 Apr. 1945
    Dear all
    Since Tuesday morning we are free!
    Riek, Lientje and I are doing fine.
    From the rest of the family no news so far.
    Best regards and until we meet again soon
    Huib, Riek, Lientje”

    Allied Losses

    On 17 September, Wing Commander P. B. Davis DSO and his crew, flying Stirling LK135 towed Horsa Glider C/N 381, which was piloted by Capt J. G. Ogilvie & Lt K. F. Strathern, both of Headquarters 'D' Sqn (Ogilvie would not return). The 20 troops they were ferrying, together with 3 motorcycles, 18 folding cycles and a pannier, were all with 9 Field Company RE. In addition to the pilot, the crew consisted of Navigator F/Lt R. Lovegrove DFC, Bomb Aimer F/Lt F. Mason, Wireless Operator F/Lt J. Francis, Flight Engineer Sgt Auld and Air Gunner F/Lt W. R. Chalk DFC. Davis and his crew were the first off from 299 Sqn, taking off at 10:15am. The tug & glider combination followed the designated Northern route towards the LZ and arrived by map reading at 13:33pm. The glider was successfully released 1 mile due south-east of the LZ at 2500ft. W/C Davis remarked that it was a “good trip”.

    The following day, W/C Davies and the same crew flew with Horsa Chalk 950 on tow on the second lift of Operation Market Garden. Again, Davis and his crew were the first off from 299 Sqn, taking off at 11:25am. This time their load was: 3 troops with HQ 4 Para Brigade, 1 jeep, 1 trailer & 2 motorcycles. The glider they were towing was headed to LZ 'X' and was released without incident.

    On the third day, the same crew and aircraft flew re-supply rather than a towing mission, and headed for DZ 'V', On reaching the DZ, Davis' Stirling took significant damage from flak. They were carrying a passenger, Squadron Leader C. A. Wingfield, a staff officer flying as an observer. The aircraft was seen going down in flames over the DZ and crashed opposite “Rust Wat” Kemperbergerweg outside Arnhem. W/C Davis, F/Lt Mason, and Wingfield died in the crash, while F/Lt's Auld, Francis, Lovegrove and Chalk were able to bail out. Bob Chalk injured his leg on landing and was soon taken prisoner. He was taken to St. Elizabeths Hospital. After a brief period of recuperation, he was sent to Stalag Luft 1, where he saw out the remainder of the war. He was discharged from the RAF on 17 October, 1945.

    German Opposition

    Corporal Rudolf Arnold served with Air Force Flak units in Germany, Romania and the Soviet Union from December 1941 to January 1943. He was then transferred to Flak unit 455 based in Germany which, on 6 June 1944, was renamed Siegfried 12500b.mot., a motorized flak ‘alarm’ group for defense against the Allied invasion in Normandy. Renamed II/ Flak Regiment 18 on 12 September 1944, it took part in defending against the Allies during Operation Market Garden. The 16th Flak Division, which included Flak Regiment 18, was credited with shooting down no less than 313 planes, including 34 gliders, during the period 1-22 September 1944, The 16th Flak Division remained based in Arnhem until 29 March 1945 when it was forced to withdraw along the Dutch-German border via Winterswijk and Oldenburg to the Cuxhaven area, where the unit eventually surrendered.

    After the war, Arnold was discharged from the Luftwaffe, the German Air Force on 17 August 1947 according to a document from the Allied 'Disbandment Control Unit'.

    War records show the regiment was deployed in the Niederrhein Luftgau VI area during 1944/45. It is adjacent to the Dutch border and close to Arnhem.

    According to the glued in section on page 28, Arnold was fighting during withdrawal from a large Dutch German area which included Arnhem. Page 38 lists his participation in later activities, although the date is covered up. It states:

    "During the air force defense artillery as Staff II. Flak regiment. 18 (mot)

    a) the defense of the air force ground operation, military, or defense economic arrangements in b) Occupied Territory HOLLAND."
